## Quick answer

If you are asking "How much can I spend today?", your bank balance is usually the wrong default. Pip answers with Spendable Cash Today, one daily spending signal after bills, protected savings, recent spending pressure, and visible cash constraints are considered.

The useful question is not "How much money exists?" The useful question is "What room do I have before the next purchase?" Pip is built to make that answer the first thing you see, then let you ask for the why when you want more context.

:::pip-says
Start with the daily number, not the biggest balance on the screen.
:::

## The real question is daily

People rarely open a money app because they want a lecture. They open it because they are about to make a choice.

Can I get lunch out? Can I buy this now? Should I wait? Should I say yes to dinner? Those are daily questions, not monthly reports.

That is why a full budget can feel too large for the moment. A budget may still be useful, but it is not always the fastest answer before a small decision. Most spending choices happen in a few seconds. The product needs to meet that moment without turning it into homework.

Pip's job is to make the daily question smaller. Open Pip, check the number, and then decide whether you need more detail.

## Why the bank balance is not enough

Your bank balance tells you what exists in an account. It does not automatically turn that account total into spending room.

The balance may include rent that has not landed yet, savings you meant to protect, card spending that has not reached checking, a subscription that is close, or a few recent purchases that changed the month. The balance can be accurate and still be a weak answer for the next purchase.

:::money-example title="Simple daily check"
Visible balance: $900
Rent soon: -$500
Protected savings: -$100
Recent card spending pressure: -$180
Daily room to consider: $120
:::

The point is not that every person has this exact math. The point is that the visible balance and the useful spending signal often answer different questions. If you use the visible balance as permission, the already-spoken-for money can catch up later.

## Pip gives the daily answer first

Spendable Cash Today is meant to be the first thing you see. It is a starting point for the next decision.

If you want more detail, Pip can explain what changed or show the consequence of a purchase. The app can answer questions like "Can I spend $50?", "Why did today change?", or "What is coming up?" without making the transaction feed the default screen.

:::comparison title="Three different answers"
Bank balance: How much exists in this account right now.
Budget category: How much was planned for a category this month.
Spendable Cash Today: What Pip sees as useful for today's spending moment.
:::

That order matters. The first answer should be the number that helps the next choice. Detail should be available, but it should not force you to become a finance analyst before lunch.

## How to use the answer

Use Spendable Cash Today as a pause point before ordinary spending. If the number is comfortable, it can make a purchase feel less like a guess. If the number is low or zero, it is a signal to slow down, ask why, or wait until the money picture changes.

The answer is most useful when you keep the habit small:

- Check Pip before the purchase.
- Treat Spendable Cash Today as the daily signal.
- Ask why if the number surprises you.
- Ask about a specific purchase when the decision is not obvious.
- Remember that missing, stale, or unconnected data can change the answer.

Pip is intentionally not trying to make every choice moral or dramatic. A lower number does not mean shame. A higher number does not mean every future obligation is guaranteed. It is a better default than the raw balance, not a promise that real life has stopped moving.

:::callout title="The useful habit"
The habit is not "review my whole financial life." The habit is "before I spend, check the daily signal."
:::

## The answer should be calm

Money tools can make every choice feel loaded. Pip tries to keep the answer short, concrete, and low-pressure.

That tone is part of the product. People are more likely to repeat a money habit when the check feels small enough to do often. The daily number should help you notice the money picture without turning a normal day into a long review session.

Pip can still be serious about trust. It should show when data is stale, when an account is missing, when a connection needs attention, or when the number has known limits. Calm does not mean vague. It means the answer should be easy to use.

## When the answer can be incomplete

Spendable Cash Today depends on the account data and settings Pip can see. If a bill is paid in cash, a shared account is missing, a transaction is delayed, or a provider connection is stale, the number can be incomplete.

That is why the answer is decision support, not financial advice or a guarantee. It is meant to improve the default moment before spending. It does not replace judgment, a bank record, a bill calendar, or a full financial plan.

If the number looks wrong, ask Pip why. If the app is missing an account, fix the connection or treat the number as lower confidence. If the decision matters a lot, slow down instead of forcing a yes or no from one signal.
