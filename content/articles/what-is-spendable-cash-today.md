## Quick answer

Spendable Cash Today is Pip's daily number for what is actually okay to use today after bills, savings cushion, and recent spending pressure are considered. It is a decision-support number, not a guarantee, not a full budget, and not the same as your bank balance.

The point is to answer a daily question: "What room do I have before I spend?" Pip makes that number the default because most everyday spending decisions happen quickly. You should not need a spreadsheet before lunch, a cart, or a night out.

:::pip-says
Your balance is real. It is just not all open room.
:::

## Definition

Spendable Cash Today is the number Pip shows first. It is meant to represent the practical room available for today's spending moment after Pip accounts for money that may already have another job.

It is not the biggest number in your bank app. It is not a monthly budget category. It is not a statement that a purchase is safe in every possible future. It is one daily signal that helps you stop guessing from a raw balance.

That distinction matters. A bank balance answers "How much exists here right now?" Spendable Cash Today answers a more useful daily question: "What can I use today without ignoring what this money still needs to do?"

## What it considers

Pip is built around account data and a protected savings cushion. The product direction is to hold back bills, savings, recent spending pressure, and other visible constraints before showing the daily number. The exact calculation can evolve as Pip learns from real usage, but the public promise should stay simple: one calm number before you spend.

:::callout title="The useful default"
Spendable Cash Today exists because the most visible number is not always the most useful number.
:::

For example, checking may show $900. That does not mean $900 is free to use. Rent may be close. Savings may need protection. Recent card spending may be waiting to hit the cash picture. Pip tries to collapse that context into a number that is easier to check in the moment.

## What it is not

Spendable Cash Today is not a guarantee. Real life can change after the number is shown. A transaction can post late, a bill can be different than expected, an account can be missing, or a provider connection can be stale. Pip should be honest about those limits.

Spendable Cash Today is also not financial advice. Pip does not tell you what your values should be, how to invest, whether to borrow, or what a purchase means for your whole financial life. It gives a daily signal from available data so you can make a more informed choice.

Finally, Spendable Cash Today is not a full budget. It does not ask you to maintain categories just to get the main answer. The product may understand transactions internally, but the user-facing habit is simpler: check one number before spending.

:::comparison title="What the number is and is not"
Spendable Cash Today: A daily spending signal before the next decision.
Bank balance: A raw account number that may include money already spoken for.
Budget: A broader plan that usually needs categories, upkeep, and review.
:::

## Why the number changes

Spendable Cash Today can change because money keeps moving. Spending lowers room. Income may raise it. Bills, transfers, card activity, and protected savings can all change the picture. Pending or newly posted transactions can make yesterday's number different from today's.

That change is part of the point. If the number never moved, it would not be reflecting the daily spending moment. Pip should make the change understandable without making the user inspect a dashboard.

When you want context, you can ask Pip. Why did today's number change? What lowered it? What is coming up? Can I spend $50? The explanation is available when you ask for it, but the number remains the first answer.

## Why it can be zero

A zero number does not mean Pip is broken. It can mean today's room is tight based on the account data and cushion Pip can see. That may happen after recent spending, before bills, when savings needs protection, or when the month is carrying more pressure than the balance suggests.

The tone matters here. Pip should not panic, shame, or turn the number into a lecture. A zero is a signal. It tells you that spending from the bank balance may be especially misleading today.

If the number seems wrong, the next step is context. Pip can explain what it sees and may point out missing data, stale connections, or account gaps. Trust comes from showing the reason, not pretending the number is magic.

## How to use it before spending

Use Spendable Cash Today as the first check before everyday spending. If the number is comfortable, you have a calmer starting point. If it is low, pause before using the bank balance as permission. If you want to test a specific purchase, ask Pip how that purchase would affect the daily number.

This is intentionally smaller than a budget ritual. It should fit into the few seconds before a decision. The habit is not "review my whole financial life." The habit is "before you spend, check Pip."
