## Quick answer

Pip is a cute daily money companion that gives you one number before you spend: Spendable Cash Today. It is made for people who do not want a budget, dashboard, spreadsheet, or lecture. Open Pip, see the daily number, ask for detail only when you want it, and move on with a calmer default.

That is the whole product promise. Pip is soft on purpose, but the job is serious. Most people do not overspend because they failed to build the perfect spreadsheet. They overspend because the number they checked right before buying something was incomplete.

:::pip-says
You do not need a finance dashboard before lunch. You need one clear number.
:::

## Why Pip exists

Most money apps ask you to become a different kind of person. They ask you to review categories, fix transaction labels, build a monthly plan, read charts, and remember which balance is the one you should trust. Some people like that. A lot of people do not.

Pip starts from a different assumption: the product should meet the daily spending moment directly. The moment is usually small. You are thinking about lunch, a cart, a night out, a quick errand, or whether you can say yes to something without making tomorrow worse. You are not trying to become a finance person. You are trying to make the next decision with a little more clarity.

The default number most people check is their bank balance. That number is real, but it is not the same as spending room. It can include rent that has not landed yet, subscriptions that are waiting, card purchases that have not cleared checking, money you meant to protect, and the pressure from recent spending.

Pip exists to turn that noisy picture into one daily signal. It is not trying to replace every financial report. It is trying to replace the misleading default people use right before spending.

## Why the companion matters

Money tools often feel heavy. Even when they are useful, they can make a simple check feel like opening a judgment machine. You see charts, warnings, red numbers, recommendations, and a lot of interface. Sometimes the app is technically correct and emotionally exhausting at the same time.

Pip is intentionally softer. The character gives the daily check a different emotional shape. Instead of making money feel like a report card, Pip tries to make the first interaction feel small, friendly, and repeatable.

That does not mean Pip treats money like a game. Cute does not mean careless. The companion is there because the habit is easier when the product feels approachable. The money signal still needs to be grounded in account data, Monthly Savings, Savings Goals, spending behavior, and clear boundaries.

:::callout title="The product idea"
Pip gets people interested. Spendable Cash Today keeps people using the app.
:::

The character and the number should work together. Pip is the hook because people can remember and like Pip. Spendable Cash Today is the habit because it answers the actual question before spending. If either part is missing, the product gets weaker. A cute character without a useful number becomes decoration. A useful number without the companion can feel like another cold finance tool.

## What Pip shows first

The first thing Pip should show is Spendable Cash Today. Not every account balance. Not a budget dashboard. Not a long transaction feed. The point is to make the useful number unavoidable and easy.

That number is the daily spending signal. It asks a different question than your bank app asks. Your bank app shows what exists. Pip asks what is actually useful today after the money has other jobs.

:::comparison title="Pip vs a traditional money app"
Traditional finance app: Start with balances, charts, categories, and cleanup work.
Pip: Start with one daily number, then ask for detail only if you want it.
:::

From there, detail is available. You can ask why the number changed. You can ask whether a purchase fits today. You can ask what lowered it, what is coming up, or why the number is zero. The explanation supports the daily number; it does not replace it as the product.

That order matters. If the first screen becomes a dashboard, Pip loses the behavior it is trying to create. The daily habit should be simple enough to repeat: check Pip before spending, understand the signal, and leave.

## What Pip does not do

Pip is not a full financial plan. It is not financial, tax, investment, credit, or legal advice. It does not promise that a purchase is safe. It does not move money. It does not ask you to maintain categories just to get the main answer.

Pip also should not shame you. A low number is not a moral judgment. A zero number does not mean the app is scolding you. It means today's signal is tight based on what Pip can see. That is useful information, especially if the alternative is checking a balance that looks bigger and spending from the wrong number.

The best version of Pip feels like a small pause before the purchase. Not a lecture. Not a spreadsheet. A pause with a clear signal.

## How Pip should feel day to day

The daily experience should be light. You open Pip and see Spendable Cash Today. If the number is comfortable, you have a calmer default. If the number is low, you know to slow down. If you want more context, you ask.

Pip should also feel honest about uncertainty. It can only work from the account data and settings it has. If a credit card is missing, if recent transactions are pending, or if an account connection is stale, the product should say so plainly. Trust comes from clear boundaries, not from pretending the number is magic.

That is why the public website needs to treat Pip as both charming and serious. The charm makes the habit feel possible. The seriousness makes account connection reasonable. Together, they create the promise: before you spend, check Pip.
